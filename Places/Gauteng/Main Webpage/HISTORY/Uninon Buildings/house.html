Union Buildings

25 Reviews

The Union Buildings are situated in Pretoria, Gauteng, South Africa. They are easily accessible via public transportation and are a must-visit for those interested in South Africa's history and architecture.

Historical Significance

Government Seat: The Union Buildings house the offices of the President of South Africa and are the site of presidential inaugurations.


Nelson Mandela's Inauguration: On May 10, 1994, Nelson Mandela was inaugurated here as South Africa's first democratically elected president, marking the end of apartheid.
Women's March of 1956: On August 9, 1956, approximately 20,000 women marched to the Union Buildings to protest against the pass laws, a significant event commemorated annually as National Women's Day.

Visiting Information

Access: While the interior offices are not open to the public, visitors are welcome to explore the gardens and monuments.
Hours: The gardens are open daily.
Admission: There is no fee to enter the Union Buildings' grounds.
Facilities: Free parking is available, and a flea market operates near the parking lot on all days except Sunday.

  • Nelson Mandela Statue A 9-meter bronze statue unveiled in 2013 stands as a tribute to Mandela's legacy
  • Louis Botha Statue An equestrian statue honoring South Africa's first Prime Minister.
  • Delville Wood War Memorial Commemorates South African soldiers who died during World War I.
  • South African Police Memoria Honors police officers who lost their lives in the line of duty.

The Union Buildings in Pretoria are a symbol of South Africa's political history and unity. Designed by Sir Herbert Baker, they were constructed between 1910 and 1913 to serve as the official seat of the South African government. Located atop Meintjieskop, the highest point in Pretoria, the buildings are an architectural masterpiece combining neoclassical, Cape Dutch, and Edwardian styles.
