Robben Island, located off the coast of Cape Town, has a long and complex history as a place of isolation and imprisonment. Initially used by the Dutch in the 17th century to banish political dissidents and enslaved people, it later became a British colonial outpost and leper colony in the 19th century. However, its most infamous role was during South Africa’s apartheid era, when it served as a maximum-security prison for anti-apartheid activists, including Nelson Mandela, who spent 18 of his 27 years in incarceration there. The harsh conditions and forced labor symbolized the brutality of the apartheid regime. After the release of the last political prisoners in 1991 and the prison’s closure in 1996, Robben Island was declared a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1997, transforming into a powerful museum and memorial. Today, guided tours led by former inmates offer visitors a poignant insight into the resilience of those who fought for freedom and justice in South Africa.
